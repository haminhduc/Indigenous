The Importance of the Land for Indigenous People and Issues with Land Rights

Land is an important part of Indigenous People’s lives since it is one of the sources of living and is connected to their spiritual beliefs, their traditional teachings and knowledge so it is a key to sustaining the culture for current and future generations of Indigenous People.

For the longest time, Indigenous land rights have been fortified through a lot of legal battles and administrative developments aimed at tackling concerns like the expansion of the land base. However, it's important to note that Indigenous rights frameworks are still far from being fully resolved and continue to be one of the most intensely debated political matters of today.

Until today, not all Indigenous groups has guaranteed the right to land. Indians with no status are not recognized as reserve members and, as a result, cannot lay claim to lands. Métis, one of the three recognized Indigenous peoples of Canada, the legitimacy of land rights' existence continues to be a subject of dispute.

Treaty renegotiation takes place in Canada today. Other groups are currently negotiating treaties for the first time – more than 70 are under negotiation as of September 2019. Furthermore, when land rights are defined, it remains to be decided on a case-by-case basis whether development projects can take place or not. The extent to which Indigenous peoples have the right to be consulted about or to veto projects in their traditional territories is disputed.

Call to Action #45

“We call upon the Government of Canada, on behalf of all Canadians, to jointly develop with Aboriginal peoples a Royal Proclamation of Reconciliation to be issued by the Crown.”

The Truth and Reconciliation Commission called on the Canadian government to use both the Proclamation of 1763 and the Treaty of 1764 as references to come up with the creation of a Royal Proclamation of Reconciliation that will be issued by the crown.

The Proclamation would include, but not limited to the following commitments:

  • Repudiate concepts used to justify European sovereignty over Indigenous lands and peoples such as the Doctrine of Discovery and terra nullius.
  • Adopt and implement the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples as the framework for reconciliation.
  • Renew or establish Treaty relationships based on principles of mutual recognition, mutual respect, and shared responsibility for maintaining those relationships into the future.
  • Reconcile Aboriginal and Crown constitutional and legal orders to ensure that Aboriginal peoples are full partners in Confederation, including the recognition and integration of Indigenous laws and legal traditions in negotiation and implementation processes involving Treaties, land claims, and other constructive agreements.

Indigenous peoples in Canada rightfully maintain custodianship of their ancestral lands, due to their historical occupation and adherence to customary law. Treaties should be respected accordingly and reevaluated as necessary. With this proclamation, it will be able to reinstate the relationship between Indigenous People and the Crown and would consider as two nations of the land and both will have rights to sovereignty. Additionally, it will recognize that Indigenous Peoples were the original settlers on Turtle Island even before the Europeans came and settled also this contradicts the Doctrine of Discovery and the concept of Terra Nullius.

Effects of Colonialism to Indigenous Land Rights

Colonialism made a massive negative effect and led to Indigenous Peoples' economic dependence and relative deprivation. Indigenous peoples have undergone a history of dispossession, facing forced displacement from their ancestral lands to make way for the expansion of settlement for the Europeans. The implementation of land allocation policies, such as the creation of reserves and the restriction of Indigenous peoples to isolated territories, has been a key factor in maintaining this situation. Indigenous Peoples were often put in Less advantageous areas with lower-quality land for agricultural and trade purposes which are far from the society or most populated areas.

Indigenous Land Rights in Canada

Land Settlements and Treaties

Treaty negotiations have been done historically and some are still under ongoing process. Most treaties recognize land rights. Making a treaty is an essential process of acknowledging formal land ownership rights for Indigenous Peoples. The Canadian government have signed agreements with different Indigenous groups between the years of 1701 and 1923 which are called historic treaties. Indigenous groups may opt to pursue legal claims or engage in negotiations for specific land claims if the government has failed to fulfill its obligations or adequately implement treaties.

Currently, numerous Indigenous groups are currently engaging in their first-ever treaty negotiations which have been signed since 1973. This shows the ongoing and unresolved nature of this issue.

The Comprehensive Land Claims Policy was accepted by the Canadian Government in 1973 and updated last 1986. This allows the Indigenous groups to negotiate agreements with the Canadian government. These agreements are called modern treaties which include the discussion about the transfer of land ownership, management of land, water, heritage, environment, and wildlife, financial compensation, sovereignty agreement (since 1995), economic development strategy, and the sharing of resource revenue.

Since 1973, 26 comprehensive land claims have been signed and are in effect, whereas at least other 56 are being negotiated as of November of 2018. Some examples of treaties under negotiation are the Innu Nation Claim (Newfoundland and Labrador), Atikamekw Nation Council Comprehensive Land and Self-Government Claims (Quebec), Algonquins of Ontario (Ontario), and Inuit Transboundary Negotiations in Northern Manitoba (Manitoba).

The Inherent Right Policy of 1995 helped the Indigenous People get their acknowledgement of the right to sovereignty, which says Indigenous People “have the right to govern themselves in relation to matters that are internal to their communities, integral to their unique cultures, identities, traditions, languages and institutions, and with respect to their special relationship to their land and their resources.” (First Nations Financial Management Board, 2018)

According to First Nations Financial Management Board, Canada has already finalized 32 modern agreements with aggregations of Inuit, Métis and First Nations, between comprehensive, stand-alone and sectoral self-government agreements and land claims (2018). Some of these modern agreements are Tla’amin Final Agreement in 2014, Eeyou Marine Region Land Claims Agreement (Quebec) in 2012, Nunavik Inuit Land Claims Agreement Quebec in 2008, Kwanlin Dun First Nation Final Agreement in 2005, Little Salmon / Carmacks First Nation Final Agreement in 1997., and many more.

Federal officials also have been coordinating with different Indigenous groups to negotiate and address their needs, rights, and interests which is called the “Recognition of Indigenous Rights and Self-Determination discussions”, which seeks to better address the needs and priorities identified by Indigenous communities and aim to bridge socio-economic disparities between Indigenous and non-Indigenous Canadians while promoting greater self-determination.
