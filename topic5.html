Indigenous comprehensive/specific land claims, ongoing negotiations

Indigenous Peoples have suffered from policies, suppression, abuse and have been deprived of basic human rights by the Canadian government for far too long. The Doctrine of Discovery issued by Pope Nicholas V in 1452, promoted the conquest, colonization and exploitation of Indigenous Peoples and their land. Pope Francis’ statement rescinding the Doctrine of Discovery could have significant implications for the land claim process and private property laws that have been predicated on this unjust assumption of ownership. The historical legal principle has been used by European “powers” to justify their colonization and appropriation of lands belonging to Indigenous peoples around the world. By repudiating the doctrine, it is likely that there will be a shift in how land claims and ownership are perceived, especially concerning Indigenous lands.

The repudiation of the Doctrine of Discovery could “bolster” Indigenous peoples' claims to access their traditional hunting grounds and land income. It could provide a legal framework to challenge the fossil-fuel development and logging that have been detrimental to Indigenous peoples’ livelihoods, cultural heritage and access to resources. In April 2023, “officials with both the fed­­eral and British Columbia governments admitted successive administrations had broken their promises to the Blueberry River First Nations, the Doig River First Nation, the Halfway River First Nation, the Saulteau First Nations and the West Moberly First Nations.” Regarding the specific case of First Nations in Canada who “signed Treaty 8 in 1899, which promised them access to their traditional hunting grounds and the ability to generate income from the land. But a protracted refusal by the province and the federal government to make good on the agreement forced the nations to spend more than two decades battling for compensation.” Loss of income, no access to traditional hunting grounds; the province had allowed “fossil-fuel development in the region which had prevented the nation from being able to live off the land.” The rejection of the Doctrine of Discovery could shift the perspective on resource extraction projects on Indigenous lands. It may prompt governments and corporations to reassess the impact of such projects on Indigenous communities, their traditional territories and the environment.

Residential schools meant to limit and eventually erase Indigenous culture, traditions, teachings, and beliefs created by the Canadian government with the help of Christian churches caused irreversible generational trauma. Children being removed from their homes and taken away from their parents resulted in their lost sense of belonging. While dealing with the confusion and lack of understanding, Indigenous children were ill-treated, disrespected, emotionally, physically and sexually abused and killed just for being who they are. “The government’s goal was to erase Indigenous Peoples and the easiest way for them to do so was mass genocide. They believed Indigenous peoples as ignorant, savages, a burden to society, and in need of guidance on the “right way to live”.” Children were not allowed to nurture, or care of parents and families; in turn, they did not learn parenting skills needed to care for and nurture their own children. Abuse learned at young ages in residential schools became parents’ methods in raising their own children. These horrific organizations and actions by people who disregarded any decency towards other human beings caused lasting effects as children are now adults, parents, and grandparents. As a result of their treatment, many Indigenous could not deal with the trauma which caused them to end their lives. Indigenous people have a higher suicide rate than other Canadians, especially women. “Some communities experience what has been called suicide epidemics.”

Residential schools and their impacts, the repudiation of the Doctrine of Discovery may serve as a symbolic acknowledgment of the injustices perpetrated against Indigenous peoples. However, this single act is nowhere close to enough to undo the lasting impacts of residential schools. Comprehensive efforts, including reparations truth and reconciliation processes and support for healing and restoration, are necessary to address and repair the intergenerational trauma and cultural suppression caused by such policies.

The repudiation of the Doctrine of Discovery could have a transformative effect on how Indigenous Peoples and their land rights are recognized and respected, but it must be complemented by concrete actions and policy changes to bring meaningful improvements in the lives of Indigenous communities.
